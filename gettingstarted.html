Resistors, Capacitors, Transistors

These components get used on a case-by-case basis in your circuit - to resist the flow of current (resistors), temporarily store electric charge (capacitors), and control two different flows of current (transistors). [Read more at the Source]

Resistors add resistance to the circuit and reduces the flow of electrical current. [Source]. It is important to use resistors to prevent burning out of parts, especially LEDs which have little internal resistance.

A capacitor is a component that stores electricity and then discharges it into the circuit when there is a drop in electricity. [Source] They ensure a steady current through the circuit - which can be helpful in ensuring constant electrical flow.

A transistor takes in a small electrical current at its base pin and amplifies it such that a much larger current can pass between its collector and emitter pins. [Source] It can then be used as just an amplifier (as used in hearing aids - where they pick up sounds and amplify them as electric currents) or also as a switch. As a switch, one small current can switch on a larger one. This is essentially how computer chips work, with billions of transistors shifting between on and off (between zero and one, essentially binary).

LEDs & Diodes

The most common function of a diode is to allow an electric current to pass in one direction (called the diode's forward direction), while blocking current in the opposite direction (the reverse direction). [Source] This means they can be placed in a circuit to prevent electricity from flowing in the wrong direction. Diodes result in a voltage drop - meaning that hooking up LEDs (also known as a "Light Emitting Diode") in a series would result in a dimmer light with each LED - although diodes do not do much to reduce the current. Therefore, it is still important to use resistors in a circuit with a diode.

Reading a Multimeter

Sparkfun: How to Use a Multimeter

If you can build a circuit, that's all fine and good, but it's always nice to be able to diagnose your own circuit (the voltage, current, etc), and be able to just test a battery. WIth a multimeter, you can check switches and power sources in your circuit to troubleshoot what might be wrong. In order to find out what's wrong, it might also be helpful to go over some basic physics, and refresh on Ohm's Law (in order to find out what resistors to use). Using these equations can help you plan out your circuits more fully before hand without endangering the more fragile parts. Still, for more basic circuits, you can always build your circuit as you go, and use a multimeter to test the current and voltage at different points.

Arduino

The Arduino is a microcontroller with a great programming system behind it - with integration with other languages like Processing. Check out the Arduino board.

Arduinos have both Digital I/O and Analog I/O ports that can be hooked up to circuits, and can be programmed to control them. Digital ports are, for the most part, pretty straightforward, and can either supply or not supply power (on or off). For example, hooking up an LED to an digital port allows the Arduino to turn it off or on by supplying or cutting off power. (However, there are ways of getting more analog-like results through the ports, using Pulse Width Modulation (PWM). )

The analog ports are not restricted to the "on-off" binary system of digital. This makes them perfect for use with sensors such as photocells or ultrasonic sensors, as their readings coming back can be interpreted as meaningful sensor data. You can read more about analog and digital signals here.
